Numerical Methods

One of the most exciting aspects of modern geophysical research is our ability to simulate physical phenomena with ever-increasing accuracy and speed. The development of easily implementable numerical methods for the solution of partial differential equations, as well as the availablity and power of modern computers, have made the solution of equations like the seismic wave equation:

\[ \rho \mathbf{\ddot{u}} = \nabla\lambda(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u}) + \nabla\mu \cdot [\nabla\mathbf{u} + (\nabla\mathbf{u})^{T}] + (\lambda + 2\mu)\nabla\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} - \mu\nabla \times \nabla \times \mathbf{u} \]

possible with even a modest laptop. Using the supercomputing resources at PICSciE, I can run these simulations for increasingly detailed Earth models, down to frequencies of 1 Hz. My research aims to harness the powers of these computational methods to further our understanding of the interior structure of the Earth, as well as other planets. In addition, I am broadly interested in numerical methods as a whole, since they exist at the fascinating intersection of mathematics, computer science, and physics.

Seismic Imaging

When you go to the doctor to get an X-Ray, high frequency electromagnetic waves are propagated through your bones and tissue to produce an image of your insides. Using the same physical principles, seismologists use much lower frequency seismic waves to produce images of the interior of the Earth. My research uses seismic data recorded by earthquakes around the globe to map out the structure of the mantle, ranging from depths near the Earth's surface all the way down to 1000 km.

In particular, I am interested in depths between 400 and 700 km, a region known as the mantle transition zone. At these depths, olivine, a major mineral constituent of much of the Earth's crust and mantle, undergoes multiple phase changes. Just as ice turns to liquid water at certain pressures and temperatures, solid olivine transforms to its high pressure forms due to the tremendous pressure at these extreme depths. Astonishingly, the signature of these phase transitions can be observed in seismic images as two bright reflectors around 410 and 660 km depth (the solid red lines in the animation). By measuring the properties of these phase transitions, we can get a sense of the variation of the temperature and pressure deep within the Earth.
